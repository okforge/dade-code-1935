1935 Miami-Dade Building Code

The 1935 Miami-Dade Building Code (formally Ordinance No. 1554) is a comprehensive municipal regulation adopted by the city-of-miami to establish minimum safety, design, and construction standards for all buildings and structures within the city limits (p. 17). Passed and adopted on August 4, 1936, the code governs the erection, alteration, repair, occupancy, and maintenance of buildings, as well as related structures like signs, awnings, and elevators (p. 17).

Scope and Administration

The code is organized into fifteen parts covering administrative procedures, definitions, occupancy classifications, fire zone restrictions, construction types, engineering regulations, and detailed building requirements (pp. 6–16). It primarily applies to new construction but includes specific thresholds for existing buildings, such as mandating full compliance or demolition if major repairs exceed 50% of a building’s value within a twelve-month period (p. 18). All structures must be maintained in a safe condition, with existing safety devices requiring inspector approval for removal (p. 19).

The code classifies buildings into Groups A through J based on their occupancy-classification and hazard level (p. 41). It also provides extensive definitions for key terms, including “Grade” (elevation relative to streets), “Story,” “Fire Wall” (continuous from foundation to roof), and “Fire Division Wall” (sub-divides building but not necessarily continuous) (p. 36, 40).

Permitting and Compliance

To ensure adherence to safety standards, the code requires building-permits for any construction or alteration work exceeding 25incost(p.20).Projectswithanestimatedcostof5,000 or more must have plans prepared or approved by a registered architect or structural engineer (p. 21). The compliance process involves verifying zoning districts, identifying applicable fire-zones, determining occupancy classification, and securing multi-departmental approvals for plumbing, electrical, and health requirements (p. 5, 20).

Fees are calculated based on a detailed table correlating occupancy groups with construction types (I-V) and cost per cubic foot (p. 25). Work commenced without a permit incurs double fees (p. 25). The building-inspection process requires permits to be posted and mandates sequential inspections for foundations, framing, stucco, and final completion (p. 26). For Type I buildings or complex designs, a “Registered Inspector” must be employed, bonded at $5,000, and present during construction until a Certificate of Compliance is issued (p. 27). A certificate-of-occupancy is required before any building can be used (p. 29).

Construction Types and Foundation Requirements

The code categorizes buildings into five construction-types, with specific regulations for Type IV (Metal Frame) and Type V (Wood Frame) structures. Type IV buildings feature steel, iron, masonry, or reinforced concrete frameworks with metal or incombustible exterior walls and roofs (p. 125). Their height is limited to forty-five feet, extending to fifty-five feet on sloping ground, with allowable areas dictated by occupancy and location (p. 126). Fireproofing of structural members is not required, and wood is permitted only where specifically allowed (p. 127).

Type V buildings utilize wood or wood-combination construction for walls, partitions, floors, and roofs, except where prohibited by fire zones or occupancy rules (p. 127). Maximum height is thirty-eight feet, or forty-five feet on slopes, with foundations required to rest on continuous masonry or reinforced concrete piers (p. 128). Exterior walls must use 2x4 studs spaced sixteen inches on centers, with thicker studs required for three-story buildings (p. 130). Detailed specifications govern exterior coverings, including weatherboarding, shingles, stucco over metal lath, and masonry veneer (pp. 132–135). Floor and roof joist spans are tabulated based on wood grade and spacing, with mandatory fire stopping every seven feet to prevent concealed air spaces (pp. 131, 135–138). Attached private garages require one-hour fire-resistive-construction for walls and ceilings, gravity-closing doors, and dedicated ventilation-requirements (pp. 139–140).

Foundations must comply with foundation-construction-requirements, mandating an 18-inch clearance between floor joists and the ground (p. 165). Wood sills and plates contacting masonry must be isolated with slate roofing or galvanized iron, while furring and vents require preservative treatment on all sides (p. 165). Approved lumber must meet specific heartwood or pressure-treated standards, explicitly excluding brush treatment except for end cuts (p. 165). Solid foundation walls require ventilated openings covered with 16-mesh non-corrosive wire, proportioned at two square feet per 15 lineal feet and distributed across at least three sides to ensure cross-ventilation (p. 165–166).

Reinforced Concrete and Engineering Standards

Chapter 26 establishes comprehensive reinforced-concrete-standards, requiring detailed drawings, reinforcement layouts, and load calculations prior to permit issuance (p. 166). Special concrete systems not covered by standard rules may be evaluated by the board-of-examiners-for-special-construction (p. 166). All materials and testing must comply with american-society-for-testing-materials standards, with the Building Inspector overseeing aggregate quality, water-cement ratios, and 28-day compressive strength verification (p. 170–173). Field testing mandates both moist-cured control cylinders and on-site job cylinders to verify mix proportions (p. 174).

The code defines allowable-working-stress limits for flexure, shear, bond, and axial compression based on concrete strength (fc′​) and reinforcement type, with spiral columns permitting higher stresses based on reinforcement ratios (p. 176–177). Concrete operations require dry excavations, continuous mixing for at least one minute, thorough compaction around reinforcement, and a minimum of seven days of moist curing, with strict cold-weather temperature controls between 50°F and 120°F (p. 178–179). Forms must be tightly sealed and braced, removable only after concrete achieves sufficient strength (p. 179–180). Reinforcement must be cleaned, bent cold without heating, and spaced with minimum concrete cover ranging from 3 inches for ground-contact footings to 1 inch for structural-fireproofing applications (p. 180–181). Construction joints must be strategically placed near mid-spans, roughened, and cleaned before resuming pours (p. 181).

Structural design relies on elastic flexure theory, assuming steel carries all tensile stress, utilizing a modular ratio of n=30,000/fc′​, and permitting a 150% increase in allowable stresses for wind-pressure-design calculations (p. 182, 184). The code specifies detailed design rules for beams, slabs, and T-beams, defining effective depth, points of inflection, and effective flange widths (p. 185). Ribbed floor systems mandate specific rib spacing and minimum slab thicknesses (p. 186). It provides explicit moment-coefficients for uniformly loaded continuous beams and slabs, differentiating between freely supported and fully restrained systems (pp. 186–188). For two-way reinforced floors, load distribution depends on panel aspect ratios, with minimum shrinkage reinforcement ratios specified by bar type (p. 189).

Shear and diagonal tension calculations dictate when shear-reinforcement such as vertical stirrups or bent-up bars must be provided, with spacing limits tied to effective depth and stress levels (pp. 189–190). Bond stress computations govern anchorage lengths, requiring sufficient embedment beyond support faces and points of inflection (pp. 191–192). Special anchorage rules apply for high shear stresses, mandating personal supervision by the designing engineer (p. 193).

flat-slabs are defined as beamless slabs reinforced in two or four directions, with standard design provisions limited to panels with length-to-width ratios not exceeding 1.33 (pp. 193–194). Moment distribution is divided between column strips and middle strips, with specific coefficients provided for slabs with or without dropped panels (p. 195). Minimum slab thickness is calculated using a formula incorporating span length, column capital size, and load intensity (p. 196). Detailed reinforcement layout rules dictate bar extensions beyond points of inflection and column heads for both two-way and four-way systems (pp. 197–198). Additional provisions address wall panels, marginal beams, and permissible openings within middle and column strips (pp. 198–199).

column-design limits unsupported lengths and lateral dimensions, with principal columns requiring a minimum 12-inch diameter or thickness (p. 200). Spiral columns permit higher axial loads based on core area and reinforcement ratio, while tied columns use gross area calculations with stricter longitudinal steel limits (pp. 200–201). Composite steel-concrete columns and long columns (slenderness exceeding 40R or 50R) include explicit reduction factors for buckling (pp. 202–204). Footings are proportioned for total column load plus self-weight, with critical bending sections located at column faces or base centers, and sloped or stepped designs permitted if shear requirements are met (p. 204).

The code also establishes comprehensive standards for structural-loads, defining dead loads as permanent structural weight and live loads as all other imposed forces (p. 140). Minimum unit live loads vary by occupancy, with reductions permitted for girders and columns based on tributary area (pp. 141–143). Owners must post designed live loads and seating capacities, and occupancy permits require verification of floor suitability (p. 144).

Structural and Operational Regulations

Beyond initial construction, the code regulates ongoing building operations and structural modifications. It establishes strict protocols for moving buildings within or into city limits, requiring performance bonds, cash bonds for street damage, continuous city inspector accompaniment during transit, and mandatory warning lights (pp. 21–22).

Enforcement powers are vested in the Building Inspector, who holds police authority to halt unsafe work, condemn dangerous structures, and mandate repairs or demolition, with costs liened against the property (p. 29). The code includes specific provisions for termite extermination (p. 31). Disputes regarding alternate materials or code interpretations may be appealed to the board-of-examiners-and-appeals, composed of an architect, builder, and structural engineer (p. 32).

For mixed-occupancy buildings, the code mandates fire-separation to prevent fire spread. Separations are classified as Absolute (4-hour resistance, no openings), Special (3-hour resistance, restricted openings), or Ordinary (1-hour resistance, self-closing doors) (p. 42).

Detailed Occupancy Standards

The code specifies rigorous requirements for assembly, institutional, and hazardous occupancies (Groups A–E), emphasizing emergency-egress, ventilation, and fire protection:

  • Group A (Large Assembly): Covers theaters and auditoriums with 1,000+ or 3,500+ seats. Mandates Type 1 construction with no height or area limits. Requires exits on three sides of the auditorium, a main foyer sized at one square foot per seat, aisles at least three feet wide, and fixed seating. Buildings with multiple balconies must include smokeproof towers. Automatic sprinkler-systems and mechanical ventilation supplying 30 cubic feet of air per minute per occupant are mandatory (pp. 45–50).
  • Group B (Medium Assembly): Applies to venues with 300–3,500 seats. Allows Types I–III construction with varying height limits. Egress rules mirror Group A but waive the foyer requirement and prohibit balconies except in Type I buildings (pp. 51–54).
  • Group C (Small Assembly): Covers spaces with fewer than 750 seats. Permits Types I–V construction with floor areas strictly tied to street frontage. Classrooms and small rooms have adapted exit rules, and schools face restrictions on placing younger grades on upper floors. Rooms over 30,000 square feet require draft stops to subdivide attic spaces (pp. 54–60).
  • Group D (Institutional): Regulates detention facilities, hospitals, and care centers. Detention buildings require Type I construction, while multi-story care facilities need one-hour fire-resistive floors and partitions. Mandates 1:6 slope ramps for bedridden patients and self-releasing panic devices on exit doors (pp. 60–63).
  • Group E (Hazardous Industrial): Governs high-risk facilities like paint storage, mills, and aircraft hangars. Imposes strict area and height caps based on construction type. Aircraft hangars are limited to 30,000 sq ft (Type I) or 15,000 sq ft (Type II), with a 100% area increase permitted if fully sprinklered (pp. 63–64).

Across these groups, the code enforces fire-separation for boiler and heating rooms, prohibits the storage of inflammable liquids outside controlled laboratory settings, and requires independent meter rooms for multi-occupancy structures. Historical sanitation provisions also mandate segregated toilet facilities based on race and gender (pp. 50, 53, 58, 62).

Means of Egress, Doors, and Windows

The code establishes strict dimensional and safety standards for stairways to ensure reliable occupant evacuation. Stairways must be at least 44 inches wide, with a maximum rise of 7½ inches and a minimum tread depth of 10 inches (p. 247). Landings at the top and bottom of every flight must be level, at least 44 inches long, and match the width of the connected stair flight (p. 247). Interior stairways require 2-hour fire-resistive-construction enclosures, while passageways serving exits must maintain a minimum clear height of 7 feet and be protected by 1-hour fire doors (p. 245). For buildings three or more stories in height, the code requires smokeproof-towers featuring 4-hour fire-resistive walls, exterior access vestibules, and continuous uniform egress to street grade (pp. 250–251). Outside stairways are permitted for buildings up to three stories or 55 feet high but may not constitute more than 50% of the required exit capacity (p. 251).

Exit enclosure doors must be self-closing, normally closed, and constructed to 1-hour fire-resistive standards, swinging in the direction of travel without obstructing stair width (pp. 245–246). First-floor plate glass windows facing public streets must be at least ¼ inch thick, limited to 91 square feet without division bars, and secured with non-corrosive metal settings (p. 252). The code mandates storm-shutter-requirements capable of withstanding 30 pounds per square foot of wind pressure for public-facing openings to protect against flying debris (p. 252). Skylights angled less than 45 degrees above the first story must be set at least one foot above the roof, and wire glass panels are restricted to 720 square inches or 48 inches in any single dimension (p. 253).

Chimneys, Heating, and Roof Structures

Exterior balconies and bays require incombustible supports, railings at least 3 feet 6 inches high, and structural design to carry a 100 psf live load (p. 254). Penthouses and roof structures housing mechanical equipment, water tanks, or stair enclosures are excluded from building height calculations but are strictly limited to 20% of the total roof area and a maximum projection of 12 to 28 feet above the roof (p. 255). Walls of roof structures within 4 feet of exterior walls must match the fire-resistive rating of the story below and project 2 feet above the roofline (p. 255).

Chimney construction requires ⅝-inch thick fire clay linings with a softening point of at least 1994°F, with all joints thoroughly slushed and grouted (p. 256). Steel smokestacks must include 25 feet of fire brick lining from the intake and external masonry or ventilated metal shielding up through the roof (p. 260). Gas vents must utilize unglazed fire clay or concrete tile pipe with sleeves at every joint, maintaining a minimum 1-inch rise per foot and strict clearances from combustibles (p. 261). Fireplaces require 8-inch thick walls, fire brick facing, and properly supported hearths extending at least 20 inches from the chimney breast (p. 263). Warm air furnaces must be double metal-shielded, installed on masonry floors, and maintain protected clearances of at least 24 inches from overhead wood framing (pp. 263–264).

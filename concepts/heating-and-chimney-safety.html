Heating and Chimney Safety

heating-and-chimney-safety encompasses the structural, material, and installation standards for chimneys, flues, smokestacks, gas vents, fireplaces, furnaces, and heating appliances. Governed primarily by Chapter 37 of the 1935-miami-dade-building-code, these regulations prioritize fire prevention, proper draft, and safe clearances from combustible materials.

Chimney Construction and Flue Linings

Chimneys must conform to standards recommended by the national-board-fire-underwriters and be designed according to local engineering requirements (p. 256). Walls must be constructed of brick, concrete, stone, or hollow tile, with a minimum thickness of eight inches (p. 259). Flue linings are mandatory, requiring fire clay or refractory materials with a softening point of at least 1994°F and a thickness of no less than five-eighths of an inch (p. 256). All joints between masonry and lining must be thoroughly slushed and grouted (p. 256). Chimneys must extend at least three feet above flat roofs or two feet above the nearest roof ridge (p. 259).

Smokestacks and Industrial Vents

Masonry and steel smokestacks used for high-temperature industrial applications must withstand wind stresses and conform to recognized engineering standards (p. 260). Steel stacks require a minimum metal thickness of one-quarter inch and must be lined with fire brick for at least twenty-five feet from the intake (p. 260). Stacks exceeding a height ten times their diameter must be properly guyed using stranded wire and turn buckles (p. 260). Industrial smokestacks mounted on boilers must maintain a six-inch clearance, with inflammable materials within twelve inches protected by asbestos and sheet metal (p. 260).

Gas Vents and Patent Chimneys

Gas appliances require vents constructed of unglazed fire clay or concrete tile pipe at least one-half inch thick, featuring sleeves or flanges at every joint to ensure secure connections and air spacing (p. 261). Vents must maintain a rise of at least one inch per foot and extend two feet above any roof portion within a fifteen-foot radius (p. 261). ventilation-requirements for gas vents mandate that vents be continuous, vertical, and clear of combustibles, with metal vent connections maintained at least six inches away from unprotected wood (p. 261). Patent chimneys, permitted for stoves but not fireplaces, require a fire clay flue lining enclosed in a metal casing with a one-inch air space, built plumb from the floor level and braced every six feet (p. 262).

Fireplaces, Furnaces, and Boilers

Fireplace walls must be at least eight inches thick, with faces exposed to fire lined with fire brick, soap stone, or cast iron (p. 263). Hearths must be supported by trimmer arches or reinforced concrete slabs, extending at least twenty inches from the chimney breast and twelve inches wider than the opening on each side (p. 263). Warm air furnaces burning solid or liquid fuel must be encased in a double metal shield with an air space, protected with three inches of sand on top, and installed on masonry floors (p. 263). Combustible partitions must be kept at least seven feet from the front and four feet from the sides of the furnace, or protected with sheet metal and asbestos paper (p. 263). Adequate combustion air supply is required, with outdoor intake openings providing a net area of at least 400 square inches (p. 264).

Low-pressure steam heating plants (up to 15 pounds pressure) and hot water heaters using solid or liquid fuel must rest upon masonry or reinforced concrete floors and be protected on the outside by asbestos (p. 265). Large boilers for power or high-pressure steam require significant clearance: no combustible material less than five feet from the top or sides, or ten feet from the front (p. 265). Combustible material closer than these distances must be protected with at least four inches of concrete, brick, or similar incombustible material, and the area must be ventilated to keep temperatures below 125°F (p. 265).

Stoves and Gas Ranges

Domestic stoves used for heating, cooking, or laundry must have combustible partitions behind and beside them protected by fire-resistive-construction (p. 265). Stoves must be supported at least twelve inches above wood floors by metal supports and placed on a metal and asbestos pad extending six inches beyond the stove on each side and twelve inches in front (p. 265). Stoves must not be placed nearer than six inches to any combustible partition (p. 265).

Gas ranges and domestic water heaters must be supported at least six inches above wood floors or combustible materials (p. 265). Where burners lack a bottom shield, wood materials must be protected with a double metal shield and a one-inch air space, or a half-inch metal and asbestos pad (p. 265). Combustible partitions within six inches of these appliances require asbestos and metal covering or one-hour fire-resistive protection (p. 266). Wood ceilings must be at least three feet above gas installations (p. 266).

Warm Air Ducts

Warm air ducts and appurtenances must be constructed of bright tin or galvanized iron (p. 267). For gravity systems, leader heat pipes shall not exceed twenty feet in horizontal length without a booster fan (p. 267). Ducts must be insulated with asbestos paper or double-walled with an air space, and horizontal pipes must be kept three inches from combustibles or protected with an asbestos shield (p. 267). Joists and studs must provide at least fourteen inches of clear space for continuous duct runs (p. 268).

Incinerators and Solar Heaters

Built-in incinerators require enclosing walls of solid masonry or reinforced concrete, at least eight inches thick for small chambers and twelve inches for larger ones (p. 268). The inner four inches of combustion chamber walls must be lined with fire brick laid in fire clay or cement mortar (p. 268).

solar water heaters must be constructed of galvanized iron or equal rust-resistant material, anchored to galvanized angle iron supports, and designed to operate under at least 100 pounds of pressure (p. 268). Tanks must be insulated with at least five inches of ground cork to prevent heat loss (p. 269).

Oil Burners

Oil burning apparatus must be constructed and erected similarly to solid fuel apparatus (p. 266). Installation must comply with regulations from the national-board-fire-underwriters and the national-fire-protection-association regarding the construction, installation, and storage of oil fuels (p. 266).

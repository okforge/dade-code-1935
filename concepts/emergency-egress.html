Emergency Egress

Emergency egress refers to the design, sizing, and arrangement of exits, aisles, stairs, and ramps that allow occupants to safely evacuate a building during an emergency. The 1935-miami-dade-building-code establishes rigorous standards for egress based on the occupancy-classification of the structure, prioritizing clear width, direct access to streets, and unobstructed travel paths.

General Requirements

All egress paths must maintain their clear width without obstruction from furniture, radiators, or fixtures (p. 49). Every building must feature a main corridor on each floor with a minimum clear width of forty-two (42) inches, increasing by one inch for each room beyond twelve up to a maximum of six feet, and must run through to an outside wall (p. 249-250). Exit doors must swing in the direction of travel, open onto a landing at least equal to the door’s width, and cannot reduce the effective stair or landing width below thirty inches when open (p. 245). If latched, doors must use self-releasing panic hardware or pressure-release locks that open upon pressure and never require a key from the inside (p. 246, 250-251). All exits must be marked with illuminated signs featuring white lights not less than five inches high on a green field, kept lit whenever the building is occupied (p. 249). Stairways and ramps must sustain a minimum live load of one hundred (100) pounds per square foot, with uniform tread and riser dimensions (variation not exceeding three-sixteenths of an inch) and slip-resistant surfaces (p. 243). All treads require a nosing of at least one inch, and width cannot decrease along the direction of exit travel (p. 243). Elevators, escalators, and revolving doors are explicitly excluded from exit calculations (p. 243). Ramps with a slope not greater than one foot in eight feet may substitute for stairways and must comply with all stairway construction, width, enclosure, landing, lighting, and ventilation requirements (p. 249).

Requirements by Occupancy Group

Egress standards scale with building size and risk:

  • Group A (Large Assembly): Requires exits on at least three sides of the auditorium, each minimum five feet wide, opening directly to a street or exit court (p. 46). A main foyer is mandatory, sized at one square foot per seat, connected by ramps with a maximum 1:10 slope (p. 46). Balconies over 50 seats require exits on both sides, and buildings with multiple balconies must include smokeproof-towers (p. 47, 49).
  • Group B (Medium Assembly): Follows Group A standards but waives the foyer requirement and prohibits balconies except in Type I construction (p. 52).
  • Group C (Small Assembly/Schools): Allows stairs instead of ramps and permits seats to remain unfixed. Small rooms (Division 3) may have all exits on one side if no occupant is more than 35 feet from an exit (p. 57). School hallways must be at least 4.5 feet wide, and single classrooms require two 3-foot doors or one 5-foot door (p. 58). Basement student areas need direct exterior exits (p. 58).
  • Group D (Institutional/Care): Mandates at least two exits per floor regardless of size. Hospitals and sanitariums require ramps with a maximum 1:6 slope for bedridden patients (p. 61). Exit doors cannot be fastened except with panic devices, except in detention facilities (p. 61-62).
  • Group E (Moderately Hazardous): Requires not less than two means of egress from each story, including basements or cellars, unless they are used solely for heating apparatus (p. 65). Automobile transfer ramps must meet the ground floor level at a point at least twenty feet from the building exit (p. 65). Smokeproof towers and other stairs comply with Chapter 33 specifications (p. 65).
  • Group F (Moderately Hazardous Industrial/Commercial) & Group G (Non-Hazardous): Both groups mandate stairs, exits, passageways, and corridors as specified in Chapter 33, with smokeproof-towers provided as required (p. 69, 72).
  • Group H (Hotels & Residential): All stairs and exits must open directly onto a street or alley, or onto a yard/court at least four feet wide that connects to a street or alley via a passageway. This passageway must match the width of the stairway opening and maintain a minimum height of seven feet (p. 75).
  • Group I (Dwellings & Small Hospitals): Stairs and exits are provided as specified in Chapter 33, with no mandatory enclosure required for stairs in these structures (p. 77).
  • Group J (Private Garages, Accessory Structures, Stadiums): Stairs and exits for amusement park devices follow Chapter 33, though stairs and ramps in buildings of two stories or fewer need not be enclosed (p. 79). Stadiums require one exit at least seven feet wide per 2,000 persons or fraction, spaced no more than seventy-five feet apart, with serving passageways maintaining a minimum seven-foot clear height and width (p. 79).

Aisles and Seating

Aisles must be free of steps or obstructions. Main floor aisles require a minimum width of three feet (seats on one side) or 3.5 feet (seats on both sides), increasing by 1.5 inches for every five feet of length toward the foyer (p. 48). No more than six seats may exist between any seat and an aisle (p. 48). In Group A buildings, seats must be securely fastened to the floor and spaced at least 33 inches back-to-back (p. 48).

For stadiums and reviewing stands in Group J, aisles must be at least three feet six inches wide, ensuring no more than twenty seats exist between any seat and an aisle (p. 79). Unmarked seating is calculated at eighteen inches per seat along a bench. Seats must be spaced at least twenty-six inches back-to-back, or thirty inches if backs are provided (p. 79-80). If the space beneath a stadium is used for other purposes, exits passing through it must be separated by walls, floors, and ceilings of at least one-hour fire-resistive-construction (p. 80). Stairs in these venues must have a rise of no more than seven and one-half inches and a tread of at least ten inches, excluding the nosing (p. 79).

Vertical Enclosures and Stairway Construction

Stairways, ramps, and elevators must be enclosed to protect escape routes. Enclosure passageways must be at least as wide as the stairway or ramp they serve, extending continuously from the lowest to highest required points (p. 238). Interior stairways must be enclosed with walls and partitions achieving a minimum two-hour fire-resistive-construction rating, with self-closing doors of at least one-hour fire-resistive rating swinging in the direction of travel (p. 245-247). Windows in these enclosures require wire glass with metal frames and sash (p. 238). Elevator shafts are limited to two elevators per enclosure, with one-hour fire-resistive walls separating distinct elevator banks (p. 238). Wood stairways must be protected on the underside by one-hour fire-resistive construction, while fully enclosed metal stairways are exempt from additional fireproofing mandates (p. 243).

Stairway dimensions are strictly regulated: minimum width of forty-four inches, maximum rise of seven and one-half inches, and minimum tread depth of ten inches (p. 247). No more than seventeen risers are permitted in a single flight between landings (p. 247). Landings at the top and bottom of every flight must be level, at least forty-four inches long, and match the width of the connected stair flight, with outer corners curved or splayed at a forty-five-degree angle to eliminate right angles (p. 247). Outside stairways are permitted for buildings not exceeding three stories or fifty-five feet in height but may not constitute more than fifty percent of the required exit capacity and must be constructed of incombustible materials (p. 251).

Roof access is integrated into egress design: in buildings three stories or higher, half of the required stairways must continue to and through the roof via a penthouse with approved openings (p. 243). Two-story buildings require 2’x3’ scuttles for roof access, and all stairways leading to roofs must be clearly marked with ground-level signs (p. 243).

Integration with Fire Safety

Egress systems work in tandem with fire-separation to protect escape routes. Stair and ramp enclosures must be fire-resistive, and vertical openings must be sealed to prevent smoke migration (p. 50, 56). smokeproof-towers are mandatory for buildings three or more stories in height across most occupancy groups, featuring four-hour fire-resistive masonry walls, two-hour floors and ceilings, and exterior access vestibules or balconies open to the street or court (p. 250-251). These towers provide continuous uniform egress to street grade via passageways with a minimum forty-four-inch clear width and eight-foot clear height (p. 251). Horizontal exits, consisting of protected openings through fire walls or bridges between buildings, offer additional refuge areas and require one-hour self-closing doors that remain continuously unlocked during occupancy (p. 249). For detailed code specifications, see 1935_Miami_Dade_Building-_Code_p45_64 and 1935_Miami_Dade_Building-_Code_p65_84.

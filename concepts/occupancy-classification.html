occupancy-classification

Properties1
descriptionCategorizes buildings into Groups A-J to dictate safety, construction, and maintenance requirements based on intended use.

Jul 07, 20268 min read

Occupancy classification is a foundational principle in the 1935-miami-dade-building-code that categorizes buildings and structures based on their intended use, which directly dictates their safety, construction, and maintenance requirements (p. 5). The city-of-miami code organizes all structures into distinct groups, ranging from Group A to Group J, each with specific regulations tailored to the unique hazards and needs of that use (p. 65). The building-inspection authority is responsible for classifying every building according to its use or character of occupancy (p. 41).

Classification Groups and Requirements

The code divides occupancies into groups covering assembly, business, residential, industrial, and institutional facilities. Once a building’s occupancy is determined, it dictates allowable construction types, maximum height and area, stair and exit configurations, light and ventilation standards, sanitation provisions, and required fire extinguishing apparatus (p. 67). This systematic approach ensures that buildings are designed to mitigate risks specific to their function.

Specific groups include:

  • Group A (Large Assembly): Theaters, auditoriums, and similar venues with 1,000+ seats require Type 1 Construction with no height or area limits (p. 45). This group also encompasses churches, libraries, museums, schools, and gymnasiums (p. 368-371). emergency-egress mandates exits on three sides, a main foyer, and smokeproof towers for multiple balconies (p. 46).
  • Group B (Medium Assembly): Venues with 300–1,000 seats allow Types I–III construction with height and area limits; foyers are waived and balconies restricted to Type I buildings (p. 51).
  • Group C (Small Assembly): Spaces with fewer than 300 seats permit Types I–V construction, with floor areas determined by street frontage (p. 54). Classrooms may have exits on one side if travel distance is under 35 feet (p. 57).
  • Group D (Institutional): Detention facilities require Type I construction, while hospitals mandate ramps with a 1:6 slope for bedridden patients (p. 60). This group also includes asylums, jails, nurseries, orphanages, prisons, and reformatories (p. 371-372).
  • Group E (Hazardous): High-risk uses like aircraft hangars, storage of flammable liquids, and public garages are restricted. This group additionally covers bus stations, dry cleaning plants, mattress factories, and planing mills (p. 368, 370-372). The code mandates at least two emergency-egress per story, with specific rules for automobile ramps and smokeproof towers (p. 65). Mechanical ventilation must supply 30 cubic feet of air per minute per occupant, and separate toilet facilities are required based on sex and race (p. 65). Dry cleaning plants require Type I construction, four-hour fire-resistive-construction partitions, and power-driven exhaust systems (p. 66). Public garages cannot be Type V construction, face strict height limits, must use incombustible floor materials, and gasoline filling stations require incombustible exterior wall coverings (p. 67). Service stations require approved grease drains (p. 67).
  • Group F (Moderately Hazardous): Includes wholesale/retail stores, offices, factories, and storage for combustible goods. This group also regulates police stations and undertaking parlors (p. 372-375). Maximum allowable floor areas are determined by construction type, building height, and street frontage, allowing a 100% area increase for complete sprinkler-systems (p. 68). Draft stops are required in rooms exceeding 30,000 square feet (p. 68). Exterior walls within four feet of property lines must be masonry or reinforced concrete (p. 68). Storage of volatile inflammable liquids is prohibited unless compliant with the 1930 National Board of Fire Underwriters ordinance (p. 69).
  • Group G (Non-Hazardous): Covers industrial and commercial occupancies with low fire and life hazards, such as ice plants, power plants, pumping plants, creameries, and storage of incombustible materials (p. 369, 371). Similar area/height tables apply, capping Type V construction at three stories and 38 feet (p. 71). Unlike higher-hazard groups, vertical openings do not require enclosure except as specified in Chapter 33 (p. 72), and under-roof fireproofing may be omitted (p. 72).
  • Group H (Residential/Hotels): Regulates hotels, apartment houses, dormitories, rooming houses, convents, monasteries, and old peoples homes (p. 369, 372). These buildings must comply with the florida-hotel-state-commission rules (p. 73). Dimensional limits are stricter, restricting Type V construction to two stories plus an attic (p. 74). Living and sleeping rooms must feature windows comprising at least 15% of the floor area (p. 75). All stairs and exits must open directly to a street, alley, or approved yard/court (p. 75). Boiler rooms require “Special fire-separation” from the rest of the building (p. 75).
  • Group I (Dwellings & Small Institutions): Applies to dwellings, garage apartments for up to two families, and small hospitals/sanitariums (p. 370, 373). Floor areas are capped at 10,000 sq ft for Types III/IV and 7,500 sq ft for Type V (p. 76). It specifies a minimum ceiling height of 8 feet 4 inches (p. 78). Strict fire-resistive-construction separation is required for attached private garages; the ceiling and walls must have a one-hour fire resistance, openings are restricted to a single doorway protected by a gravity-closing solid wood or metal door without glass, and dedicated exhaust ventilation openings are required near the floor (p. 77, 139-140).
  • Group J (Accessory & Amusement): Covers private garages, sheds, stadiums, amusement park structures, tents, towers, and reviewing stands (p. 368, 375). Spectator venues require specific stair dimensions, exit widths (one 7-foot-wide exit per 2,000 persons), and aisle spacing (every 20 seats) to ensure safe emergency-egress (p. 79). Private garages attached to residential buildings need fixed louvered or exhaust ventilation, and direct openings into living/sleeping rooms are banned (p. 80). Garages storing over three cars require chemical extinguishers (p. 80).

Vertical Transportation and Special Apparatus

Occupancy classification dictates the requirements for elevators, escalators, dumbwaiters, and hoists, which are critical for multi-story buildings across various groups. The code mandates that all such lifting apparatus undergo regular inspection and receive a certificate of operation from the Building Inspector (p. 365-366). Ordinance No. 2149 establishes that inspection reports must be filed by either city employees or approved insurance/casualty company inspectors (p. 365). Fees for these inspections are set at $2.50 plus 50 cents for the certificate, though the inspection fee is waived for approved private inspectors after July 1, 1939 (p. 366). These regulations ensure that vertical-transportation-safety is maintained regardless of whether the apparatus serves a public assembly, a hospital, or a commercial warehouse (p. 369).

Structural Load Requirements by Occupancy

Occupancy classification directly governs the structural-loads a building must support. The code establishes minimum unit live loads in pounds per square foot based on the intended use, ensuring structural adequacy for varying occupancies. For example, dwellings and hospital wards require 40 psf, offices require 50 psf, retail stores require 75 psf, and manufacturing or heavy storage facilities require 125 psf or more (p. 141-142). Additionally, allowable floor areas for construction-types like Type IV and Type V buildings are explicitly capped based on their assigned occupancy group and location within the city (p. 126, 128).

Common Standards and Site Requirements

Regardless of the specific occupancy group, the code enforces baseline standards for sanitation, ventilation, and site placement. ventilation-requirements dictate that all habitable spaces receive natural light/air via windows or skylights covering at least 1/8 of the floor area, or a mechanical system providing a specified air exchange rate (p. 69, 375). Buildings employing more than four people must provide toilets appropriate for their occupancy group, with specific requirements listed for Groups A through J (p. 375). Furthermore, every occupancy group is subject to mandatory set-back regulations to ensure adequate spacing from property lines and public ways (p. 373). sprinkler-systems and standpipes are consistently referenced across groups to mitigate fire risks (p. 66). Across all groups, the code mandates independent central meter rooms for multiple-occupancy buildings (p. 67, 70, 73, 76, 78).

Mixed Occupancies and Fire Separation

Buildings housing multiple uses are subject to strict regulations regarding mixed occupancies. When different portions of a building are placed in different occupancy Groups, a fire-separation must be provided to entirely segregate each group per Section 503 (p. 42, 67, 70, 73, 76, 81). These separations are classified as:

  • Absolute: 4-hour resistance, no openings allowed.
  • Special: 3-hour resistance, restricted openings with self-closing doors.
  • Ordinary: 1-hour resistance, openings protected by self-closing metal-clad doors (p. 42). A detailed tabulation specifies the required separation levels between various groups to prevent fire spread (p. 44).

Changes in Use

Changing the occupancy of an existing building triggers significant compliance obligations. No change in the character of occupancy shall be made without a certificate-of-occupancy permitting the new use (p. 41). If a use changes to one that would not be permitted in a newly erected building, the entire structure must be brought up to code, unless the new use is segregated or remains within the same occupancy group (p. 19). Furthermore, any change in occupancy requires a revised occupancy permit certifying that the building’s floors are structurally suitable for the live loads characteristic of the proposed use (p. 144).

Integration with Building Permits & Fire Zones

Determining the correct occupancy classification is a critical step in the building-permits process. Applicants must specify the intended use for all parts of a building when filing plans, and this classification works alongside fire-zones to establish the complete regulatory framework for a project (p. 5). The code divides the entire city into three fire zones: Zone 1 covers the densest commercial and residential areas near the miami-river and biscayne-bay (p. 81-82), while Zone 2 extends north along major arteries like federal-highway and biscayne-boulevard (p. 83-84). Stricter construction and relocation standards apply within these zones, particularly for Types IV and V buildings. As detailed in 1935_Miami_Dade_Building-_Code_p5_24, proper classification ensures that structural, fire, and life-safety standards align with the building’s operational risks.

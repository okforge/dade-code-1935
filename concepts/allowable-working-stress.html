Allowable Working Stress

Allowable working stress defines the maximum unit stress permitted for structural materials under combined live and dead loads, ensuring a factor of safety against failure. In the 1935-miami-dade-building-code, these limits are strictly regulated for masonry-standards, wood-framing-standards, reinforced-concrete-standards, and structural steel to account for material variability and construction methods.

Masonry Standards

Under Section 2410, allowable compressive stresses for masonry vary by material type, unit strength, and mortar composition.

  • Brick: Stresses range from 90 to 400 psi depending on the unit’s average compressive strength (1,250 to 4,500 psi) and the mortar type (Lime, Lime-Cement, or Cement) (p. 150-151).
  • Concrete: Plain concrete allows for compressive stresses up to 0.20 of the ultimate strength (fc′​) and shear stresses of 0.02 fc′​ (p. 151).
  • Block and Tile: Concrete block or tile and load-bearing clay tile are limited to 80 psi gross cross-sectional area when laid in cement mortar, or 70 psi when laid in lime-cement mortar (p. 151-152).
  • Stone: Rubble stonework is limited to 140 psi (Portland cement mortar) or 100 psi (lime-cement mortar). Ashlar masonry limits are higher, reaching 800 psi for granite in Portland cement mortar (p. 153).
  • Concentrated Loads: Concentrated loads may exceed allowable working stresses by no more than 25% (p. 153).

Solid masonry walls must maintain these compressive stresses within code limits under combined live, dead, and wind loads (p. 224). Wall thickness scales with building height, and lateral support via reinforced concrete tie-beams and coping is mandated to resist wind forces and ensure structural continuity (p. 223-224).

Wood Standards

Wood stresses are determined by the quality grade of the lumber, categorized as “Structural,” “Yard,” or “Other” lumber (p. 154-156).

  • Structural Lumber: Must be grade-marked and graded according to american-society-for-testing-materials or lumber association rules. Allowable stresses for bending, shear, and compression are provided in detailed tables for species such as Douglas Fir, Southern Pine, and Oak (p. 156-157).
  • Columns: The allowable compressive stress for wood columns depends on the slenderness ratio (unsupported length to least dimension, l/d):
    • Short Columns (l/d≤10): Use standard compressive stress values (p. 159).
    • Intermediate Columns: Use a parabolic reduction formula until the stress reduces to one-third of the short column value (p. 159).
    • Long Columns: Use the Euler formula, with a maximum slenderness limit of l/d=50 (p. 159).

Gypsum

For gypsum used in structural applications (floor/roof construction), allowable working stresses vary by composition. Neat gypsum has higher allowable stresses (e.g., 350 psi extreme fiber compression) compared to gypsum fiber concrete or coarse aggregate concrete, which range from 100 to 220 psi depending on the percentage of wood chips or aggregate (p. 152).

Reinforced Concrete

Allowable stresses for reinforced-concrete-standards are calculated based on the minimum ultimate 28-day compressive strength of the concrete (fc′​) and the modular ratio n=30,000/fc′​ (p. 176, 182). The code specifies distinct limits for flexure, shear, bond, and axial compression:

  • Flexure: Extreme fiber compressive stress in concrete is limited to 0.40fc′​ for general cases and 0.45fc′​ adjacent to supports of continuous beams or rigid frames (p. 176). For standard strength grades, these values range from 800 to 1,350 psi (p. 176).
  • Shear: Permissible shear stress on concrete (vc​) depends on the presence of web reinforcement and the anchorage of longitudinal steel. Values range from 0.02fc′​ (no web reinforcement, no special anchorage) to 0.09fc′​ (with web reinforcement and special anchorage) (p. 176). Flat slabs and footings have specific shear limits based on anchorage conditions (p. 176).
  • Bond: Allowable bond stress (u) varies by bar type and member geometry. Deformed bars permit higher bond stresses (up to 0.05fc′​) compared to plain bars (0.04fc′​) in beams and slabs, with reduced values for two-way footings (p. 177). Special anchorage provisions allow doubling these bond values (p. 177).
  • Axial Compression: Concrete in columns is limited to 0.25fc′​ for general axial compression and 0.225fc′​ for columns with lateral ties (p. 177). Spirally reinforced columns permit higher stresses, calculated as 300+(0.10+4p)fc′​, where p is the ratio of longitudinal reinforcement, allowing stresses up to 1,320 psi for high reinforcement ratios (p. 177).
  • Reinforcement Steel: Tensile stress limits for intermediate grade billet steel and rail steel bars are capped at 20,000 psi, while web reinforcement is limited to 16,000 psi (p. 177). Compression stresses in steel bars are limited to nfc′​, with structural steel and cast iron sections in composite columns capped at 15,000 psi and 9,000 psi, respectively (p. 177).
  • Wind Load Adjustments: When designing for wind loads, allowable unit stresses for both concrete and reinforcement may be increased to 150% of the standard values, provided the structural section meets minimum requirements for dead and live loads alone (p. 184).

Unreinforced concrete pedestals are strictly limited to a maximum compressive stress of 0.25fc′​ on their gross area, with depth-to-width ratios capped at three times the least width to prevent buckling (p. 206).

Structural Steel and Iron

Allowable unit stresses for structural steel and iron are strictly defined to prevent failure under tension, compression, bending, and shear. Rolled steel allows 18,000 psi for tension and compression on short lengths, while cast steel permits 16,000 psi, and cast iron is limited to 10,000 psi in compression (p. 207). Column design incorporates slenderness ratios (l/r), with maximum stresses capped at 15,000 psi for structural steel columns and governed by specific formulas for steel pipe and cast iron columns (p. 207-208). Bending stress on extreme fibers is limited to 18,000 psi, with reductions for laterally unsupported lengths exceeding 15 times the flange width (p. 208). Shear limits range from 13,500 psi for power-driven rivets to 12,000 psi for beam webs, with bearing stresses capped at 30,000 psi for double shear and 24,000 psi for single shear (p. 208-209).

welded connections have distinct allowable stresses: 13,000 psi for tension in butt welds, 11,300 psi for shear in fillet welds, and 15,000 psi for compression (p. 213). Combined stresses from wind and other loads require adherence to specific permissible working stress multipliers, and members subject to stress reversal must be designed for the largest section plus 50% of the smaller stress (p. 209).

Foundations and Piles

Soil bearing capacities dictate foundation design, with limits set at 5 tons per square foot for solid rock, 2.5 tons for natural bed sand, and 0.5 tons for filled land (p. 219-220). Pile foundations must adhere to strict load limits based on material and driving methods. Concrete piles are limited to a maximum working stress of 12% of the concrete’s ultimate compressive strength, while concrete-filled steel tubes allow up to 25% plus 7,500 psi for the steel component (p. 221-222). Wood piles are capped at 20 tons for untreated piles and 15 tons for creosoted piles, with allowable loads further calculated using hammer drop or steam hammer penetration formulas (p. 221). Load tests for piles require sustaining twice the design load for 48 hours, with settlement limits tied to the test load and structural continuity (p. 222).

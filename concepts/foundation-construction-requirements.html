Foundation Construction Requirements

Foundation construction requirements establish the structural, material, and environmental standards necessary to ensure the stability and longevity of building bases. These regulations govern excavation, soil bearing, wood preservation, ventilation, concrete placement, and pile foundations to protect against moisture, decay, and structural failure. Amendments to the code further refine these requirements, specifically addressing curing times for masonry and structural details for low-height walls and concrete columns.

Excavation and Soil Bearing

Excavations must be protected against danger to life and property, utilizing retaining walls of masonry, reinforced concrete, or interlocking steel piling for permanent cuts (p. 219). Excavations cannot extend within one foot of the angle of repose under existing footings unless properly under-pinned (p. 219). Footings and foundations must be constructed of masonry, reinforced concrete, or structural steel encased in concrete, with masonry units laid in Portland cement mortar (p. 219).

Soil bearing capacities are strictly regulated: solid rock allows up to 5 tons per square foot, rock with cleaned pot holes filled with concrete allows 3 tons, natural bed sand allows 2.5 tons, and filled land allows only 0.5 tons (p. 219–220). When soil bearing capacity is uncertain, load tests are mandatory, requiring an area of at least two square feet to sustain twice the desired bearing capacity for 48 hours without additional settlement (p. 220). Foundations should rest on natural solid ground; filled land or organic soil is restricted to single-story Type V construction (p. 220).

Pile Foundations

When natural ground is insufficient, pile foundations must be driven to a reasonably solid bearing without eccentric loading (p. 220).

  • Wood Piles: Must be free of decay, driven green, and meet specific taper and diameter requirements (minimum 6-inch point, 10-12 inch butt depending on length) (p. 220). Untreated piles must be cut below mean low water or groundwater levels. Creosoted piles (Douglas fir or Southern pine) meeting american-wood-preservers-association standards may be cut above ground if properly capped and treated (p. 220–221). Allowable loads are calculated via drop or steam hammer formulas, capped at 20 tons for untreated and 15 tons for creosoted piles (p. 221).
  • Concrete Piles: Must comply with material and reinforcement standards. Allowable working stress cannot exceed 12% of ultimate compressive strength, except for steel-tube cast piles which allow up to 25% plus steel tube stress, capped at 50 tons (p. 221–222). Precast piles require minimum 1.2% longitudinal reinforcement and specific hooping, with length limits tied to diameter (p. 222).
  • Load Testing: Piles may require load testing to twice the design load for 48 hours, with settlement limits of 1/100th of the test load in tons for continuous structures (p. 222).

Wood Foundation Standards

Wood components in contact with masonry or concrete must be isolated using eighty-five-pound slate roofing or twenty-four-gauge galvanized iron to prevent moisture transfer (p. 165). Approved lumber must possess physical properties equal to eighty-five percent Heart Long Leaf Southern Yellow Pine, No. 1 Common Tidewater Red Cypress, or other specified heartwoods and pressure-treated materials (p. 165). Brush treatment is prohibited except for end cuts (p. 165). Wood furring and foundation screen vents require preservative treatment on all four sides (p. 165).

Floor joists must maintain a minimum clearance of eighteen inches above leveled ground to facilitate airflow and prevent rot (p. 165). Wood posts or columns cannot rest directly on concrete floors; they require concrete floatings extending at least two inches above the finished floor or corrosion-resistant metal plates (p. 165). Timbers embedded in masonry walls below ground level must be enclosed in metal wall boxes and coated with coal tar creosote (p. 165).

Foundation Ventilation

Adequate airflow beneath structures is critical for moisture control. Solid foundation walls under first-floor joists must include ventilated openings covered with non-corrosive wire mesh of at least sixteen mesh per linear inch (p. 165–166). Ventilation area must total at least two square feet for every fifteen linear feet of exterior wall, distributed across a minimum of three sides to ensure cross-ventilation (p. 166). These requirements align with broader ventilation-requirements to prevent dampness and structural degradation.

Foundation Wall Specifications

Foundation walls must extend below the frost line and cannot be constructed of gypsum (p. 236). Solid masonry and concrete block or coursed stone foundation walls require a minimum thickness of ten inches, unless otherwise specified (p. 236). To support superimposed loads, foundation walls must provide at least a four-inch bearing surface for wood joists and eight inches for unit masonry, resulting in a general minimum thickness of twelve inches (p. 236). Exceptions allow for an eight-inch minimum in Type V construction or when exterior masonry walls rest on engineered reinforced concrete floor slabs supported by a reinforced concrete foundation wall (p. 236).

Cast-in-place concrete foundation walls must be at least as thick as the walls they support, with an absolute minimum of eight inches (p. 236). Rubble stone foundation walls require a minimum thickness of sixteen inches, with height restrictions applied based on construction quality: rough or random rubble without bonding or level beds is prohibited for walls exceeding thirty-five feet, while coursed bonded rubble is limited to seventy-five feet (p. 236). Foundations for hollow clay tile, concrete block, or hollow brick walls must match the thickness requirements for solid masonry, with tile foundation walls requiring a minimum of twelve inches (p. 236).

If earth pressure and building loads exceed the maximum allowable working stresses for the chosen materials, wall thickness must be increased accordingly (p. 236). Materials must equal the quality required for exterior bearing walls, utilizing cement mortar for below-grade sections (p. 236). Additionally, masonry unit foundations must be sealed below any woodwork with a cement wash or equally effective seal to prevent moisture migration (p. 236).

Code amendments specify that all foundations for masonry construction must be allowed to cure for 12 hours before any masonry elements are laid (p. 334). For specific low-height structures, such as fences, planting box walls, or walls up to four feet in height, foundations must be at least ten inches wide and eight inches deep, reinforced with 2.5-inch rods (p. 334). U-Type Beam Blocks are permitted with an unsupported maximum span of seven feet, requiring reinforcement of two 7/8-inch rods placed at the top and bottom of the beam (p. 336).

Concrete Foundations, Pedestals, and Reinforcement

Concrete foundations and footings must adhere to strict reinforced-concrete-standards. Later updates to the code explicitly adopt the Building Code Requirements for Reinforced Concrete ACI 318-47 as the standard for reinforced concrete work (p. 336). Metal reinforcement in footings and principal structural members contacting the ground requires a minimum concrete cover of three inches (p. 181). Other surfaces exposed to weather or ground require at least two inches of cover (p. 181). Formwork must be removed only after concrete achieves sufficient strength, and all wood forms within the ground or less than eighteen inches above it must be removed before occupancy (p. 166, 180).

Concrete placement demands clean excavations, proper mixing, and continuous pouring to minimize construction joints where possible (p. 178, 181). Curing requires exposed surfaces to remain moist for at least seven days, with additional precautions in hot or freezing weather (p. 179). Quality control involves field testing, compression tests, and adherence to specified water-cement ratio limits to guarantee structural integrity (p. 173–174).

Pedestals and footings require careful load distribution analysis, including bending moment calculations for trapezoidal load areas (p. 205). Reinforcement spacing and pitch must be maintained with secure spacers, and lateral reinforcement ratios are capped at 0.015 (p. 206). Unreinforced pedestals are limited to a maximum compressive stress of 0.25fc′​ and strict depth-to-width ratios, with projections limited to half the depth (p. 206).

Concrete columns are required at all corners and at specified intervals. These columns must be at least eight inches thick and twelve inches wide, reinforced with four 5/8-inch continuous vertical rods and 1/4-inch ties spaced on 12-inch centers. U-Type Blocks are explicitly prohibited for reinforced concrete column use (p. 336). Structural integrity at gable ends is ensured by an eight-inch by eight-inch reinforced concrete tie beam constructed similarly to a coping beam, required unless the rake and height are less than 24 inches (p. 338).

Inspection and Compliance

All foundation work is subject to building-inspection protocols. Inspectors verify material quality, reinforcement placement, concrete curing, and adherence to clearance and ventilation mandates (p. 170, 178). Contractors must maintain detailed records of material tests, mixing proportions, and placement dates, which remain on file for two years post-acceptance (p. 170, 179). Pile inspections may require certification from approved testing laboratories to verify conformity with creosote treatment and driving methods (p. 221). These regulations are detailed in 1935_Miami_Dade_Building-_Code_p165_184, 1935_Miami_Dade_Building-_Code_p205_224, and 1935_Miami_Dade_Building-_Code_p325_344, and form a critical component of the 1935-miami-dade-building-code.

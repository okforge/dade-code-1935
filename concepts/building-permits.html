Building Permits

Building permits are the primary regulatory mechanism used by the city-of-miami to authorize the erection, alteration, repair, moving, or demolition of buildings and structures. Under the 1935-miami-dade-building-code, no construction work exceeding $50 in cost for repairs, or any remodeling or alteration job of any value, may commence without first obtaining a permit from the Building Inspector (p. 333).

Application Requirements

Applicants must submit a written application detailing the property location and intended use or occupancy-classification (p. 20). The application must be accompanied by duplicate sets of scaled plans (minimum 1/8 inch to the foot) and a lot plan showing existing and proposed structures (p. 20). Plans must be stamped and approved by multiple city divisions, including Plumbing, Electrical, Zoning, and Public Health, depending on the project scope (pp. 20–21). Additionally, the Building Inspector may require specific approvals from the Fire Department and the Department of Public Service for street lines and grades, which must be stamped directly onto the plans (p. 334). To ensure worker safety and sanitation, all construction sites lacking water-washed facilities must provide flyproof privies with at least two private stalls (p. 334).

Recent amendments have tightened application standards to prevent property disputes and ensure compliance. Ordinance No. 3827 mandates that all permit applications for new structures, additions, or building moves must include a registered land surveyor’s certificate and plan (p. 350). This survey must detail property corner stakes, line dimensions, existing utilities, elevations, rights-of-way, easements, setbacks, and zoning records (p. 350). Furthermore, Ordinance No. 3965 stipulates that permit applications will generally be accepted only from licensed contractors (p. 360). An exception exists for sole owners applying for permits to construct, repair, or alter their own private residences for personal occupancy (p. 360). Maintenance, repairs, or additions costing less than $2,500 are also exempt from the contractor licensing requirement, unless they involve structural changes (p. 360).

Professional Oversight

For projects with an estimated cost of $5,000 or more, plans and specifications must be prepared and sealed by a registered architect or structural engineer licensed in Florida (p. 21). The professional must certify that the plans fully comply with the code, and plans cannot be reused for different locations without revision (p. 21).

Fees, Bonds, and Valuation

Permit fees are calculated based on the estimated cost of construction, determined by multiplying the building’s cubic content by a schedule of cost-per-cubic-foot rates categorized by building Group and Type (pp. 325–330). These fee schedules were significantly updated by Ordinance No. 2972 in 1945 and further amended by Ordinance No. 4049 in 1950 to align with actual increased building costs (p. 330). The Building Inspector is required to maintain a permanent, accurate ledger of all fees collected, including payer names, dates, amounts, and property locations (p. 327). If construction begins or proceeds before the permit is officially issued, the required fees are doubled, though payment does not exempt the violator from other penalties or code compliance (p. 327). Government entities remain exempt from permit fees but must still secure the necessary documentation (p. 24).

Specialized Structure Permits

Beyond standard building construction, the code mandates specific permitting processes for ancillary structures and mechanical systems.

  • Signs and Billboards: Permits are required for projecting signs over five feet in length, as well as for bulletin boards, poster boards, and roof signs (p. 307). Applications must include detailed drawings specifying location, construction materials, weight, illumination methods, and wind stress compliance (p. 307). Additionally, any individual or business manufacturing or painting signs must secure a separate license (p. 307).
  • Elevators and Escalators: Installation or alteration of vertical transportation systems requires a triplicate application with accompanying plans, subject to vertical-transportation-safety standards (pp. 311–312). No system may enter service until it passes a rigorous building-inspection and receives a formal certificate of approval (p. 312).
  • Awnings and Tents: Erecting, altering, or removing temporary-canvas-structures over public property requires a permit and approved plans when demanded by the Building Inspector (p. 315). Special permits are also necessary for tents pitched within designated fire-zones or for canopies protecting multiple vehicles (pp. 315–316).

Moving Buildings

Relocating a structure within or into city limits requires a separate written permit from the City Manager, contingent on recommendations from the Building Inspector and Director of Public Service (p. 21). Issuance, Compliance, and Appeals

Once plans are verified and fees paid, the Building Inspector stamps the documents “Approved” and issues the permit (p. 22). One approved set must be retained on-site throughout construction and remain open to inspection (p. 22). Upon completion of the work, the owner must secure a certificate of occupancy before the building can be used (p. 5). If a party disagrees with a condemnation or permit decision, they may appeal to a Board of Appeals within five days of the final ruling, with the board’s decision being final (p. 321).

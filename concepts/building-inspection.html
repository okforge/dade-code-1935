Building Inspection

Building inspection is a critical regulatory process designed to ensure that construction, alteration, repair, and demolition activities comply with established safety standards and building codes. Under the 1935-miami-dade-building-code, the chief-building-inspector holds broad authority to enforce provisions, enter premises, halt unsafe work, verify material quality, and manage permit issuance (p. 29, p. 145, p. 347).

Permit Requirements & Site Preparation

Before any construction begins, strict administrative and site preparation standards apply. Applications for building-permits must now be accompanied by a registered land surveyor’s certificate and plan detailing property corners, line dimensions, interior angles, existing structures, utilities, elevations, rights-of-way, easements, and zoning records (p. 350).

Permit applications are generally accepted only from contractors licensed in their respective classifications. Exceptions are made for sole owners performing or supervising work on their own private residences, as well as for maintenance, repairs, or additions under $2,500 in value, provided no structural changes are involved (p. 360). Additionally, a permit card must be posted conspicuously on the premises before work commences (p. 26).

To protect public infrastructure, the Chief Building Inspector requires a performance bond or cash deposit prior to permit issuance for construction, excavating, or grading (p. 347). If damage occurs or debris is left behind, the Inspector can notify the responsible party to correct conditions within 48 to 5 days; failure to comply allows the city to use the deposit for repairs and cleanup, refunding any remainder after final inspection (p. 348, p. 363). Appeals regarding deposit amounts are directed to the department-of-public-service (p. 348, p. 362).

Inspection Process

The Building Inspector conducts mandatory inspections at key stages:

  • Foundation Inspection: Conducted after trench excavation and form erection (p. 26).
  • Frame Inspection: Performed once roofing, framing, fireblocking, and bracing are complete (p. 26).
  • Stucco Inspection: Required before stucco application, once lathing and materials are ready (p. 26).
  • Final Inspection: Completed when the building is ready for occupancy, preceding the issuance of a certificate-of-occupancy (p. 26).

No work may proceed past an inspected stage without written approval, and structural elements cannot be concealed without inspector authorization (p. 26).

Specialized System Inspections

Beyond standard structural phases, the code mandates rigorous oversight for vertical transportation, signage, and exterior protections:

  • Elevators and Escalators: The Building Inspector must inspect passenger and employee elevators at least once every three months (p. 314). If deemed unsafe, the Inspector can halt operations until repairs are made. Inspections may also be conducted by other competent authorities if accepted by the Inspector (p. 314). Furthermore, Ordinance No. 2149 (p. 365) mandates that all inspection reports, whether filed by city employees or private insurance or casualty company inspectors, be submitted directly to the Building Inspector (p. 365-366). Based on these reports, the Inspector issues operation certificates valid for three months for passenger and freight elevators, and six months for dumbwaiters (p. 366). The fee structure is set at 2.50forinspectionand0.50 for the certificate, with a provision to waive the inspection fee for approved private inspectors after July 1, 1939 (p. 366). All fees collected are directed to the Tax Collector of the City of Miami rather than a special elevator fund (p. 367).
  • Signs and Billboards: Should any sign be found unsafe, insecure, or non-compliant during inspection, the owner has forty-eight hours to rectify the issue. Non-compliance results in removal at the owner’s expense (p. 308).
  • Awnings and Canopies: Installation, repair, or alteration of awnings, canopies, and tents over public property requires a permit and strict adherence to structural standards, with ongoing oversight to ensure safety against wind stress and fire hazards (p. 314).

Material Testing & Quality Control

The Building Inspector possesses the authority to mandate structural and fire-resistive material testing whenever there is reason to believe materials do not meet code requirements, and may require retesting if quality degrades over time (p. 145). All material tests must be conducted in accordance with american-society-for-testing-materials (ASTM) standard specifications (p. 145, p. 170). The financial responsibility for these tests falls on the Owner, General Contractor, or Manufacturer (p. 145).

Manufacturers may register identifying symbols for their products with the Inspector to facilitate verification, though this does not waive construction or testing requirements (p. 148). Additionally, for “Yard” or “Other” lumber that lacks standardized grade marks, the Inspector must visually inspect the material, assess defects, and assign allowable unit working stresses based on comparisons to American Lumber Standards (p. 154).

For reinforced concrete projects, the Inspector’s oversight extends to rigorous field testing and quality assurance. The Inspector may require concrete specimens to be taken at the point of deposit, cured, and tested per ASTM standards to verify mix quality (p. 170). During construction, at least one compression test specimen is required for every 100 cubic yards of concrete placed, or one per 50 cubic yards if specific material tests were used to establish the mix (p. 173, p. 174). If test results indicate that the 28-day strength falls below the planned minimum, the Inspector has the authority to mandate load tests, order changes to the water-cement ratio, or require adjusted curing conditions (p. 173, p. 174).

Architect & Engineer Supervision

While the Building Inspector enforces code compliance, the code mandates that all concrete work be actively inspected by the responsible Architect or Engineer, or a competent representative (p. 170). This professional supervision requires maintaining a detailed record of material quantities, water quality, mixing procedures, reinforcement placement, and ambient temperatures below 40°F (p. 170). These records must be preserved for two years and remain available for the Building Inspector’s review at all times (p. 170). Furthermore, concrete cannot be placed until the forms and reinforcement have been specifically inspected and approved by the Architect or Engineer (p. 178). Splices of reinforcement at points of maximum stress also require explicit Inspector approval (p. 180).

Load Testing

The Inspector retains the right to order load tests on any completed structural portion if conditions raise reasonable doubt about its adequacy (p. 170). Such tests cannot be conducted on concrete less than 60 days old (p. 170). The structure must withstand a superimposed load equal to 1.5 times the live load plus 0.5 times the dead load for 24 hours (p. 170). Failure is defined by evident structural compromise or a deflection recovery of less than 75% within 24 hours after load removal; failing structures must be modified or re-rated (p. 170).

Registered Inspectors & Special Supervision

For Type I buildings or projects with complex designs and new materials, the code mandates a “Registered Inspector” (p. 27). This individual must be bonded, constantly present on-site, and responsible for material verification, defect reporting, and code enforcement (p. 27-28). Their duties conclude only upon the issuance of a Certificate of Compliance (p. 28).

Dangerous Buildings & Enforcement

The Building Inspector plays a central role in identifying and mitigating dangerous-buildings and public nuisances. Dangerous structures are defined by criteria including structural listing, significant deterioration (33% of supporting members or 50% of enclosing walls), inadequate egress, fire hazards, and substandard sanitation or overcrowding, assessed using standards developed by the american-public-health-association (p. 352-353).

The Inspector is tasked with semi-annual inspections of public assembly buildings and annual inspections of all city buildings to identify violations (p. 354). Upon discovering a dangerous condition, the Inspector places a conspicuous notice on the structure and issues a written directive via registered mail to all interested parties, outlining required repairs, vacation, or demolition (p. 354-355). Standards dictate that buildings be ordered repaired if feasible, vacated if immediately hazardous to occupants, or demolished if 50% damaged or unrepairable (p. 354).

The Fire and Police divisions must report suspected dangerous buildings to the Inspector within 24 hours of discovery (p. 358). If an owner disputes the Inspector’s decision or fails to comply within the mandated timeframe (typically 30 to 60 days), the matter is escalated to the building-board-of-appeals (p. 355-356). This five-member board, composed of master builders, structural engineers, and architects, conducts hearings, makes written findings, and issues final orders for repair, vacation, or demolition (p. 356-357). Non-compliance with board orders can result in city-mandated action, with costs liened against the property or pursued legally (p. 357).

Violations of inspection and enforcement provisions are punishable by fines up to $500, imprisonment up to 60 days, or both, with each day constituting a separate offense (p. 348, p. 359). The Inspector also retains specific duties regarding pest control; upon discovering termite infestations, the Inspector must notify the owner or agent in writing and order extermination within a reasonable timeframe, not exceeding sixty days (p. 163).

Historically, applicants disputing code interpretations or enforcement actions could appeal to the board-of-examiners-and-appeals (p. 32), but specialized dangerous building appeals are now handled by the dedicated Building Board of Appeals to ensure expert review of structural and safety condemnations (p. 356).

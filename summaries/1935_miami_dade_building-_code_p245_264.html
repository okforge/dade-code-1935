Overview

This document contains excerpts from the 1935 Miami-Dade Building Code, covering Chapters 33 through 37. It establishes comprehensive regulations for emergency-egress, fire-resistive-construction, structural elements, and mechanical installations, reflecting early 20th-century priorities in urban fire safety and building durability.

Stairways and Egress Requirements

The code mandates strict dimensional and safety standards for stairways to ensure reliable occupant evacuation. Stairways must be at least 44 inches wide, with a maximum rise of 7½ inches and a minimum tread depth of 10 inches (p. 247). Landings at the top and bottom of every flight must be level, at least 44 inches long, and match the width of the connected stair flight (p. 247). Interior stairways require 2-hour fire-resistive-construction enclosures, while passageways serving exits must maintain a minimum clear height of 7 feet and be protected by 1-hour fire doors (p. 245). For buildings three or more stories in height, the code requires smokeproof-towers featuring 4-hour fire-resistive walls, exterior access vestibules, and continuous uniform egress to street grade (p. 250-251). Outside stairways are permitted for buildings up to three stories or 55 feet high but may not constitute more than 50% of the required exit capacity (p. 251).

Doors, Windows, and Skylights

Exit enclosure doors must be self-closing, normally closed, and constructed to 1-hour fire-resistive standards, swinging in the direction of travel without obstructing stair width (p. 245-246). First-floor plate glass windows facing public streets must be at least ¼ inch thick, limited to 91 square feet without division bars, and secured with non-corrosive metal settings (p. 252). The code mandates storm-shutter-requirements capable of withstanding 30 pounds per square foot of wind pressure for public-facing openings to protect against flying debris (p. 252). Skylights angled less than 45 degrees above the first story must be set at least one foot above the roof, and wire glass panels are restricted to 720 square inches or 48 inches in any single dimension (p. 253).

Balconies, Penthouses, and Roof Structures

Exterior balconies and bays require incombustible supports, railings at least 3 feet 6 inches high, and structural design to carry a 100 psf live load (p. 254). Penthouses and roof structures housing mechanical equipment, water tanks, or stair enclosures are excluded from building height calculations but are strictly limited to 20% of the total roof area and a maximum projection of 12 to 28 feet above the roof (p. 255). Walls of roof structures within 4 feet of exterior walls must match the fire-resistive rating of the story below and project 2 feet above the roofline (p. 255).

Chimneys and Heating Apparatus

Chimney construction requires ⅝-inch thick fire clay linings with a softening point of at least 1994°F, with all joints thoroughly slushed and grouted (p. 256). Steel smokestacks must include 25 feet of fire brick lining from the intake and external masonry or ventilated metal shielding up through the roof (p. 260). Gas vents must utilize unglazed fire clay or concrete tile pipe with sleeves at every joint, maintaining a minimum 1-inch rise per foot and strict clearances from combustibles (p. 261). Fireplaces require 8-inch thick walls, fire brick facing, and properly supported hearths extending at least 20 inches from the chimney breast (p. 263). Warm air furnaces must be double metal-shielded, installed on masonry floors, and maintain protected clearances of at least 24 inches from overhead wood framing (p. 263-264).

Key Themes

The document emphasizes building codes as a systematic approach to mitigating urban fire risks, enforcing material durability, and standardizing egress pathways. It reflects the era’s engineering standards for wind resistance, structural load distribution, and mechanical ventilation safety.

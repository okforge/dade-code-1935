descriptionRegulations for Groups A-E building occupancies in the 1935 Miami-Dade Building Code.

Jul 06, 20264 min read

This document covers Chapters 6 through 10 of the 1935 Miami-Dade Building Code, establishing detailed safety, construction, and egress standards for occupancy-classification from Groups A through E.

Group A: Large Assembly Occupancies Group A covers theaters, auditoriums, and similar venues with seating capacities of 1,000 or more (Division 1) or 3,500 or more (Division 2) (p. 45). These structures must use Type 1 Construction and face no restrictions on height, area, or seating capacity (p. 45). emergency-egress requirements are stringent: exits must be provided on three sides of the auditorium, each at least five feet wide, opening directly to a street or exit court (p. 46). A main foyer is mandatory, sized at one square foot per seat, with ramp slopes limited to 1:10 (p. 46). Aisles must be at least three feet wide, with no more than six seats between any seat and an aisle (p. 48). Fixed seats are required, and exit doors must swing outward with self-releasing panic hardware (p. 48-49). Buildings with multiple balconies require smokeproof towers on each side (p. 49). Ventilation must supply 30 cubic feet of air per minute per occupant, and sprinkler-systems are mandatory (p. 49-50).

Group B: Medium Assembly Occupancies Group B includes similar assembly spaces with 300–1,000 seats (Division 1) or 750–3,500 seats (Division 2) (p. 51). Height and area limits vary by construction type (I, II, or III), with Type I allowing unlimited height and up to 3,500 seats (p. 51). Exit requirements largely mirror Group A but waive the foyer requirement and prohibit balconies except in Type I buildings (p. 52). fire-separation and sprinkler-systems follow similar protocols (p. 53-54).

Group C: Small Assembly Occupancies Group C covers venues with fewer than 300 seats (Division 1), 50–750 seats (Division 2), or fewer than 50 seats (Division 3) (p. 54). These buildings may use Types I through V construction, with maximum floor areas strictly determined by building height and street frontage (p. 54-55). Rooms exceeding 30,000 square feet require tight draft stops to subdivide attic spaces into 20,000-square-foot sections (p. 55). Exit rules are adapted for classrooms and small rooms, allowing exits on one side if no point is more than 35 feet from an exit (p. 57). Schools face additional restrictions on upper-floor placement for younger grades (p. 59).

Group D: Institutional and Care Occupancies Group D encompasses detention facilities, hospitals, sanitariums, and orphanages (p. 60). Division 1 (detention) mandates Type I construction throughout, while Division 2 requires one-hour fire-resistive floors and partitions for multi-story buildings (p. 60-61). emergency-egress provisions include mandatory ramps with a maximum 1:6 slope for bedridden patients (p. 61). Exit doors cannot be fastened except with self-releasing panic devices (p. 61-62). Ventilation and fire-separation standards align with previous groups, with strict prohibitions on volatile liquid storage (p. 62-63).

Group E: Hazardous Industrial and Commercial Occupancies Group E regulates high-risk facilities like paint storage, planing mills, gasoline stations, and aircraft hangars (p. 63). Floor area and height limits are tightly constrained by construction type and street frontage (p. 63-64). Aircraft hangars are specifically capped at 30,000 square feet for Type I and 15,000 square feet for Type II, with a 100% area increase permitted if fully sprinklered (p. 64). Draft stops are again required for rooms over 30,000 square feet (p. 64).

Common Requirements Across all groups, the code mandates fire-separation for boiler rooms and heating plants, prohibits inflammable liquids except in controlled laboratory quantities, and requires independent meter rooms for multi-occupancy buildings (p. 50, 54, 60, 63). Historical sanitation provisions note segregated toilet facilities based on race and gender (p. 50, 53, 58, 62).
